This map visualizes the banana trade flows and trade balances among South American countries. Trade routes are represented by arrows, whose thickness and color intensity indicate the volume of bananas traded, with darker and thicker arrows signifying larger quantities. Countries are colored according to their net banana trade balance: orange tones denote net exporters, and blue tones indicate net importers. Circular symbols represent gross trade volume at key locations, with larger circles highlighting significant trade hubs. The map employs an Albers Equal Area projection tailored for South America.

The countries receiving the most banana imports (attracting flows) are primarily located in the southern part of South Americaâ€”Argentina and Chile stand out clearly as major importers. Conversely, Ecuador and Colombia in the northern region are the largest exporters, sending substantial volumes southward.

The dominant direction is north to south. Banana trade predominantly moves from tropical, banana-producing countries in the north (Ecuador, Colombia) towards the temperate southern regions (Argentina, Chile). Secondary flows occur eastward and southward from other smaller exporting countries.

The flow directions clearly follow geographic and climatic conditions. Tropical northern countries (Ecuador, Colombia) with favorable conditions for banana cultivation act as exporters (highlighted in orange on the choropleth), while southern, temperate countries lacking extensive banana cultivation import heavily, represented in shades of blue. Node sizes emphasize this trade dynamic: larger nodes in importing countries underline their substantial reliance on external banana sources.
